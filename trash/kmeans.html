K-means Clustering Algorithm

Introduction to K-means

    K-means is the most basic (and one of the fastest) clustering algorithms.
  1. The program begins by dropping k cluster centers that are randomly dropped into the plane.
  2. In the first step of the iterative process, the algorithm creates a temporary cluster by assigning every point the closest "cluster centers."
  3. The second step of the iterative process holds all the points and their labeled clusters constant and moves each cluster center to the center of mass of the clusters.
  4. The program ends when each cluster center no longer moves, or moves an insignificant amount.

Can Kmeans Produce a "Human" Output?

To show an application of K-means, let's look at a clustering of the population of the United States. If the cluster of lights should correspond to the population distribution of the United States, then it would be interesting to see if K-means would cluster the United States the same way a human would. Some questions we can ask are:

  • Can K-means can pick out large region such as?
    • New England
    • Mid-Atlantic
    • West Coast
    • The South
    • And so forth.
  • Can K-means pick out smaller regions such as?
    • NY tri-state area
    • California Bay Area
    • Sprawling Cities like Dallas

Data Processing

Starting with a google search for "US at night", I found a nice 9MB satellite image. With some basic image processing, I stripped away the colors of this image and calculate the location of each light pixel. Since the data is so large (almost 1 billion pixels of light), I chose a very small sample (0.01%) of the data to represent the US. Bright areas have high chances of yielding light pixels while darker areas have small chances of yielding light pixels.

Methdology

I found a nice set of clustering algorithms from Scikit.org . In addition to K-means they also provide packages for Gaussian Mixtures (a more accurate formulation of the problem), Mini-batch K-means (a faster algorithm at the cost of slight inacuracy), and Affinity Propogation (strong for numerous, uneven clusters).

K-means is a fast algorithm with low memory constraints. My laptop yields a converged simulation in about ~10 seconds on four 2.6 Ghz i7 processors.
